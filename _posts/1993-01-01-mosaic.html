Excerpt

The web created by Tim Berners-Lee was academic: interlinked research documents built on text. Images were popping up in different windows, there were no colors and no design on web pages.

Enter Marc Andreessen and his friend Eric Bina. They have created the first popular browser, Mosaic, pulling the web out from academic environment to desktop.

Now everybody could surf the web on PC/Mac as we do it today. There was no need for mainframes and cryptic operating systems. Mosaic became the killer application the web needed and led to the first internet boom in the nineties.

Netscape

Nobody thought but it turned out Andreessen has talent in business not just in technology. With Bina and a former founder of Silicon Graphics, a supercomputer giant they've started a new company Netscape, meant to become famous in web history.

Netscape was, well, one of the first web startups.

The problem was hard. Take the code from Mosaic, build a product which is still open source but also commercially viable, and face the competition springing up from the mushrooming Mosaic clones.

The product became a success—de-facto standard for web browsing. At it's peak in 1996 Netscape Navigator had a stunning 80% market share.
The freemium business model they've invented—a combination of free liceneces for personal, and paid licence for commercial use—also became a standard on the internet and used even today.

To became an internet legend and a 21st century cyber hero Andreessen needed a Goliath—services offered by then-tech-god Microsoft.

Microsoft also built a browser based on Mosaic, the infamous Internet Explorer, and planned to roll out a monopoly over the web, as did it with Windows in desktop operating systems.

Netscape and Microdoft started the first browser and involved everybody from web producers to web surfers. Some websites worked well in a browser but broken in another; Internet Explorer came preinstalled with Windows making people think there is no alternative to browsing; developers and designers had nightmare building for Explorer since Microsoft overruled the web standards; the cost of cross-browser compatible websites doubled or even tripled during the war.

Andresseen won. Today, after ten years Microsoft is non-significative in web when the successor of Netscape, The Mozilla Foundation is a cornerstone. Andreessen sold Netscape to AOL for a stunning $4.2bn and established himself as one of the most respectable figures in the web business with Twitter, Facebook, Pinterest, Github & co. in his investment portfolio.
