Gaming and Animation

In the nineties with the success of Windows many PCs turned into game stations. Remember Wolfenstein 3D and Doom?
As the browser emerged into a new universal platform the need to make it gaming friendly was an urge.

Game programmer Johnatan Gay saw this opportunity first and created a web environment where desktop-like animations were possible.

Flash and multimedia

His product and company was acquired by creative software giants Macromedia and then Adobe making it de-facto standard for web based interactive media.

Flash was fresh air for web, a killer application which made everybody happy. Advertisers could have now animated banners, web designers and developers could create cartoon-like websites, games were everywhere, scientists show us magic with animated visualizations, print designers could embed their best of typography into websites, and the possibilities were endless.

Web content become rich, all types of users were satisfied. It became obvious anything can be done with this new platform. After ten years 96% of all browsers were using Flash and there were 2 million professionals creating content with.

The End Of Flash

There are some success stories which come to and end. More often those who ignore the basic rules. Flash, from inception violated the first rule of the internet, the openness. It was like a black box inside the web browser: nobody knew what was inside. Even Google could not figure out what a Flash site is and penalized them in search results. Being closed source —with no community behind it's code base—the mobile revolution took on surprise Flash, and it's parent company Adobe. The black box grew over the tiny device and even a powerful company couldn't save this wonder technology from disappearance.
